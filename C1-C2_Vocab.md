Here are the 20 advanced verbs explained, with examples, and related business situations:

To advocate (1:34)

Meaning: To publicly support or recommend a particular policy or action.
Formal Example: The organization will advocate for stronger environmental regulations in the upcoming legislative session.
Informal Example: I always advocate for getting enough sleep before a big exam.
Business Situation: A company might advocate for tax incentives for renewable energy, or a sales team might advocate for a new software solution to a client.
To amplify (1:57)

Meaning: To make something louder or stronger; to increase the effect or impact of something.
Formal Example: The new marketing strategy aims to amplify the brand's presence across digital platforms.
Informal Example: Can you amplify the music a bit? I can barely hear it.
Business Situation: A social media campaign is designed to amplify customer testimonials, or a manager seeks to amplify the team's successes during a quarterly review.
To articulate (2:18)

Meaning: To express or explain something in a clear and effective way, or to pronounce words clearly.
Formal Example: The CEO was able to articulate a compelling vision for the company's future during the investor call.
Informal Example: I struggled to articulate my feelings after the argument.
Business Situation: A good presenter must articulate complex data into understandable insights. Employees need to articulate their needs effectively to HR.
To comprehend (2:45)

Meaning: To understand the meaning or significance of something.
Formal Example: It is essential that all employees comprehend the new safety protocols.
Informal Example: I just can't comprehend why he would do something like that.
Business Situation: Managers need to ensure their teams comprehend project objectives. Legal teams must comprehend intricate contract clauses.
To convey (3:05)

Meaning: To communicate or express something (often a message or information) to others.
Formal Example: The annual report serves to convey the company's financial performance to stakeholders.
Informal Example: How do I convey to him that I'm really upset without sounding angry?
Business Situation: A marketing team needs to convey the unique selling points of a product. Leaders must convey clear expectations to their subordinates.
To corroborate (3:25)

Meaning: To confirm or support a statement, theory, or finding with evidence or testimony.
Formal Example: Additional data was needed to corroborate the initial findings of the market research.
Informal Example: His alibi was corroborated by his friend who was with him.
Business Situation: Internal audits corroborate financial records. Witness statements might corroborate an incident in a workplace investigation.
To decipher (3:49)

Meaning: To discover the meaning of something written in a bad or hidden way (e.g., code, symbols, or difficult handwriting).
Formal Example: Analysts worked to decipher the encrypted messages exchanged between the rival companies.
Informal Example: I spent an hour trying to decipher my doctor's messy handwriting on the prescription.
Business Situation: Data scientists decipher complex datasets to find trends. IT security personnel decipher unusual network traffic.
To differentiate (4:17)

Meaning: To distinguish or recognize the differences between things or people.
Formal Example: The company aims to differentiate its product through superior customer service.
Informal Example: It's hard to differentiate between identical twins without their distinct personalities.
Business Situation: Businesses differentiate their products from competitors. HR departments differentiate between candidate skills during recruitment.
To dissect (4:37)

Meaning: To analyze or examine something in detail, often by breaking it down into its components or parts.
Formal Example: The consultants will dissect the company's operational processes to identify inefficiencies.
Informal Example: Let's dissect what went wrong with that presentation, step by step.
Business Situation: Financial analysts dissect quarterly reports. Product development teams dissect user feedback to refine features.
To efface (5:00)

Meaning: To remove or erase something intentionally so it is no longer visible or noticeable.
Formal Example: The company attempted to efface its controversial past from public memory.
Informal Example: He tried to efface the embarrassing mistake from his mind.
Business Situation: A company might try to efface negative publicity through new branding initiatives or public relations campaigns.
To elaborate (5:20)

Meaning: To add more detail or explanation to something, often to expand or clarify a concept, idea, or thought.
Formal Example: The speaker was asked to elaborate on the implications of the new economic policy.
Informal Example: Could you elaborate a bit more on what you mean by "early"?
Business Situation: During a meeting, a team member might be asked to elaborate on their proposal. Customer service agents often elaborate on product features.
To exacerbate (5:44)

Meaning: To make a situation or problem worse.
Formal Example: The unexpected supply chain disruption served to exacerbate the production delays.
Informal Example: Arguing about it will only exacerbate the tension between them.
Business Situation: Poor communication can exacerbate team conflicts. A market downturn can exacerbate financial difficulties for struggling companies.
To extrapolate (6:03)

Meaning: To guess, estimate, or forecast something using what you already know (e.g., from past data or trends).
Formal Example: Based on current sales figures, we can extrapolate a significant increase in revenue by year-end.
Informal Example: You can't just extrapolate from one bad experience that all restaurants are terrible.
Business Situation: Market researchers extrapolate future consumer behavior from current trends. Financial planners extrapolate investment growth based on historical performance.
To facilitate (6:24)

Meaning: To make something possible or easier or more convenient to do.
Formal Example: The new software system will facilitate seamless collaboration among remote teams.
Informal Example: Could you facilitate a meeting between me and the manager?
Business Situation: Project managers facilitate communication among team members. Technology can facilitate more efficient workflows.
To galvanize (6:40)

Meaning: To shock or excite someone into taking action.
Formal Example: The recent downturn in market share served to galvanize the sales team into aggressive action.
Informal Example: Her passionate speech galvanized the crowd to volunteer.
Business Situation: A crisis can galvanize a company to innovate. A strong leader can galvanize employees to achieve ambitious goals.
To integrate (6:58)

Meaning: To combine or blend things together in a harmonious or functional way.
Formal Example: The company plans to integrate the newly acquired software into its existing IT infrastructure.
Informal Example: It's hard to integrate new ideas into an old, rigid system.
Business Situation: Companies integrate new employees into the corporate culture. Mergers require careful integration of two different business operations.
To mitigate (7:16)

Meaning: To make something less harmful, less unpleasant, or less bad; to reduce or alleviate the severity or impact of something.
Formal Example: Risk management strategies are designed to mitigate potential financial losses.
Informal Example: We need to find a way to mitigate the stress of exams.
Business Situation: Cybersecurity measures mitigate the risk of data breaches. Disaster recovery plans mitigate the impact of unforeseen events.
To perceive (7:42)

Meaning: To think of something in a particular way, or to become aware or conscious of something through your senses or intuition.
Formal Example: The public perceives the brand as a leader in sustainable technology.
Informal Example: I perceived a strange smell when I walked into the room.
Business Situation: A company's brand image is how consumers perceive it. Management needs to perceive potential issues before they escalate.
To reconcile (8:05)

Meaning: To make people become friends again after an argument, or to find an acceptable way of dealing with two ideas or concepts that seem opposed.
Formal Example: The mediator helped the two departments reconcile their differing approaches to the project.
Informal Example: After weeks of not speaking, they finally reconciled.
Business Situation: Accounting teams reconcile financial statements. HR might reconcile employee disputes.
To scrutinize (8:40)

Meaning: To examine or inspect something very closely, very thoroughly, often combing through for mistakes.
Formal Example: The audit committee will scrutinize all financial transactions for any irregularities.
Informal Example: My mom always scrutinizes my room before I have friends over.
Business Situation: Legal teams scrutinize contracts for loopholes. Quality control departments scrutinize products before release.
